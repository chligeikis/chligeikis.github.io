Research Overview: Throughout my MS and PhD studies I have conducted both analytical and experimental research, spanning the fields of structural dynamics, control systems, energy harvesting, mechatronics, and uncertainty quantification. The list below briefly summarizes the major research projects I've worked on as a graduate student and includes links to relevant publications.

  • 1) Self-powered feedback control systems: A self-powered system is a control actuation technology that derives all energy to power its operations from the dynamic response of the plant in which it is embedded. In the context of structural control of buildings or bridges, such systems are attractive as they do not rely on any external power supply, which may be unreliable during extreme events such as earthquakes. The simplest example of a self-powered control system consists of an electromechanical transducer embedded within a vibratory system and electrically connected to an energy storage subsystem (e.g., a supercapacitor). In this configuration, the transducer is able to absorb energy from the vibrating structure (by converting mechanical energy to electrical energy), store that energy in the supercapacitor, and then intelligently re-inject that stored energy at a future time to reduce the vibratory response. More generally, a network of several transducers could be embedded within a single structure (or in different structures) and share power with each other via a single storage system. The synthesis of feedback control laws for use with self-powered systems is challenging, as they must explicitly account for parasitic losses in the transducers, power electronics, and energy storage subsystem. Given this context, my dissertation research has focused on:

    • Identification of theoretical conditions that guarantee self-powered feasibility of feedback control laws [W1,J2,C6]
    • Development of tractable approaches for the design of both linear and nonlinear self-powered feedback controllers [C7]
    • Experimental validation of a prototype self-powered structural control system using real-time hybrid simulation (RTHS) [W3] [W4]

  • 3) Uncertainty quantification via real-time hybrid simulation: RTHS is a cyber-physical vibration testing method that partitions a structural system into numerical and physical substructures. RTHS incorporates sensing, actuation, and computing technologies to couple these substructures in real time such that the full system performance may be studied. The method can be used to realistically examine the dynamics of structural systems with rate-dependent components that may be difficult to accurately model. However, uncertainty can be found in many aspects of RTHS testing including noise in sensor measurements, actuator performance and tracking, non-linearities in the physical test specimen, and numerical modeling assumptions. In the numerical substructure, uncertainty in material properties, stiffness, damping, or geometry may be considered random variables. Certain physical substructure characteristics may also be similarly parameterized. My research aimed to develop and experimentally validate techniques that incorporate these parametric uncertainties into RTHS testing protocols, thereby improving the robustness of the RTHS method. This work included:

    • Experimental validation of a structural reliability method known as Adaptive-Kriging Hybrid Simulation (AKHS) on a vibratory system with 24 random parameters [W1,J2,C6]
    • Development and validation of a method to identify metamodels of a system’s frequency response functions using principal component analysis, Kriging, and RTHS [C7]
    • Collaboration with researchers at ETH Zurich to experimentally quantify the buckling probability of an aluminum plate with uncertain boundary conditions using AKHS and psuedodynamic hybrid testing [W3] [W4]
